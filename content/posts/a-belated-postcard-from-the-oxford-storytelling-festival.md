As soon I saw that Sara Liisa Wilkinson was programmed into two slots at this years's [Oxford Storytelling Festival](https://www.oxfordstorytellingfestival.co.uk/2023-programme), there was no debate about whether to attend or not...

{{< figure src="/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/oxford-setting.jpg" alt="" caption="" >}}

Friday was get in day, with an opening celebration of sorts (drumming — meh) and Jan Blake set that was moved by the weather out of the amphitheatre...

{{< figure src="/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/oxford-amphitheatre.jpg" alt="" caption="" >}}

...and into the tipi.

{{< figure src="/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/oxford-tipi.jpg" alt="" caption="" >}}

The only tale I recall I'd heard earlier in the summer at the Three Bishop's first Sunday open mic in Brighstone, when a visiting storyteller to the island, Keith Rogers, (a regular of the [Birmingham Storytelling Cafe](https://www.birminghamstorytellingcafe.co.uk/), I believe?), told the same Arabian tale, in a more evocative way, about an old woman bettering the devil (iblis) in terms of achieving mischief; _the old woman brings a happy man — a cloth-seller — down, by tricking him into thinking his new new wife is unfaithful, by buying some material from him, ostensibly for her philanderer son's lover, and spilling some tea on it, which the cloth-seller finds in his house. The old woman then repairs the damage by saying she lost the material in the house of a young woman who gave her some water..._

Martha Tilston then provided a musical close to the formal programme for the Friday evening, with a suitably folky and silky voiced musical set.

The formalities complete, an "open mic" fireside session followed.

{{< figure src="/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/oxford-fire-circle.jpg" alt="" caption="" >}}

The only tale I recall, and that I've told out since (though I messed up the maths!), from someone whose name I did not get, was of a merchant who died leaving instructions to his three sons to split the estate three ways, with a 1/2 to the eldest, a 1/3 to the middle son, and 1/9 to the youngest. All went well until it came to dividing the 17 camels. As the brothers were working themselves up into a state about how to fulfil their father final wishes, one of his old friends, wtih an old (incontinent, as I have since told it!) camel tuend up and asked what was causing their distress. He offered to help if they granted him three favours: first that they accept his camel as a gift, second, that they return to him anything of his that his that that they no lonfger required; third, that if ever he was passing and asked them for hospitality, they would fee him well. The rest of the story plays out naturally...

I'm not sure what when on at the Wolf tent...

I'd only ever heard Sara Liisa on podcasts and podcast style recordings before, (a telling of [The Devil's Violin](https://soundcloud.com/user-571633720/episode-24-the-devils-violin). which I have run with a couple of times, and [The Golden Balls](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xVLjtO4EkFQ) (e.g. from Jacob's [More English Fairy Tales](https://archive.org/details/moreenglishfairy00jacoiala/page/12/mode/2up)), and was immensely looking forward to _Strange Girls Beautiful Monsters_... and was not disappointed.

From the programme, the Wolf Tent and the Yurt were all a bit woo woo workshop for me, and I'm not sure what went on. I'm definitely and the performance/entertainment end of the spectrum, and for me personally the holistic/thereapeutic personal storytelling end of the spectrum is one of the ad blurb things that would put me right off if I didn't know stories were a more entertaining night out than that...
